Introduction

¶

Crime rates are known to vary across geographic locations and the underlying cause of this variation are many and disputed. Of all these causes, could employment be a strong one? Do higher employment rates increase the crime rate or decrease it? Does time have any sort of factor in either of these topics? These are some of the questions we hope to explore in this tutorial.

Our tutorial serves to analyze the potential relationship between the rate of employment and the average crime rate. We want to analyze this question in a small controllable group since attempting to analyze this relationship across the whole country may have many other factors that affect both employment and crime rates. We have chosen to focus our study within the state of Maryland. While different locations could provide different results we hope that starting with one area could give some useful starting insight. We hope to take our data for crime rates and employment and compare and contrast them to provide a coherent and cohesive study of how the two may relate to each other.

In this tutorial, we will present our datasets for employment and crime rate and modify them so that they can be used to provide an analysis of the potential relationship between crime rate and employment over the same period of time. We will show figures depicting any potential correlation between unemployment and crime rate as well as figures demonstrating the change in the unemployment rate and crime rates separately over time. This data will then be used to perform a hypothesis test which will attempt to understand any potential relationship between unemployment and crime rates. Additionally, as another regression algorithm, decision tree classification will be used in addition to the linear regression model to analyze potential relationships in the given data.

This tutorial utilizes Python 3 and its associated libraries as well as datasets containing employment rate and crime rate information in the state of Maryland. Becoming familiar with these libraries and their associated function will be useful to follow along with this tutorial. These libraries include Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib, Statsmodels, and Scikit-learn.

Analysis¶

The two categories of the crime dataset being analyzed above concern the average crime rate over time and the percent change in crime rate over time. Upon further analysis, it becomes clear that both Figure 1 and Figure 2 describe similar overall trends.

Figure 1, displaying crime rate between the years 2007 to 2019, shows an overall decreasing trend. The crime rate appears slightly increase to a peak crime rate in 2008 and then gradually decreases. Figure 2 concerns the percent change in crime over time. A positive value indicates an increase in crime rate compared to the previous year while a negative value indicates a decrease in crime rate compared to the previous year. The year 2008 sees the only significant instance of an increase in crime percentage in Maryland from 2007-to 2019. While there are instances where the percentage change is closer to zero (indicating little change as compared to the previous year) there are no other instances of a positive change.

In both graphs, it can be seen that, overall, crime rates have steadily decreased in Maryland after peaking in 2008. It is possible that this peak in crime rates can be attributed to economic issues in the midst of the 2008 financial recession, a potential factor that will be analyzed more in the upcoming sections of this tutorial.

Regression Analysis

In this step, our most significant regression model is that of the ordinary least squares regression for crime and unemployment rate. This regression model attempts to analyze a potential relationship between the average unemployment rate and average crime rate, with the average crime rate as the dependent variable. The model found an r-squared value of .950, indicating that 95 percent of the variation in the average crime rate can be attributed to the variation in the unemployment rate average. The p-value found by the ANOVA conducted by the regression finds a p-value of <.001, indicating that the results of the model are statistically significant. Here we can determine a positive linear relationship between the average unemployment rate and average crime rate.

Conclusion

In this tutorial, our main goal was to analyze the possible potential relationship between crime rates and employment in the state of Maryland. We began by considering the dataset that we planned to use. Our first step was to make sure to tidy up the data so we could use what was relevant for our analysis. We did this by filtering both the crime and employment dataframes to include only the years between 2007 and 2019 since the datasets overlapped over this timespan. In addition to this, columns of the dataframes that were not relevant to our analysis were removed for clarity purposes. After plotting the datasets to analyze the rates of average crime and unemployment rates over time as well as analyzing them side by side, we were able to draw a few conclusions. In relation to time, the crime rate dropped consistently over the years, and there seemed to be a similar trend with the unemployment rate. This alone was not enough to create a relationship between the two variables but it did aid in giving us the insight that time had an effect on both of these things in a similar manner. There was an interesting observation we were able to make; in 2008 there seemed to be a large spike in the percent change of crime and variance of employment. This leads us to believe that there must’ve been some sort of event that caused such a drastic change. From basic background knowledge, we can hypothesize that this event was the 2008 Recession. Because of the aforementioned trend that both the unemployment rates and crime rates spiked simultaneously around this time, we were provided with an inkling of some sort of relation between the two variables.

Here, our exploratory analysis portion was helpful since it pointed to a potential relationship between crime rate and unemployment rate as shown in the figure below. This provided the foundation for performing a worthwhile regression analysis on these variables.

Our first regression model that demonstrated a significant relationship between the variables we are analyzing was the ordinary least squares regression for crime and unemployment rate. The regression analysis provided an r-squared value of .927, indicating that 92.7 percent of the variation in crime rate per 100,000 people can be explained by the variation in the average unemployment rate. Our associated p-value for this regression is <.0001, indicating that the results of the model are statistically significant.

Our second regression model was the ordinary least squared regression for crime and unemployment rate variance. The analysis of variance is unique since it is a measure of spread. In this case, the variance measures the dispersion of unemployment rates. This regression yielded an r-squared value of .267, indicating that not much of the variation in the crime rate per 100,000 people can be explained by the variation in the unemployment rate variance. Our associated p-value was found to be .0584. This is greater than .05. This, coupled with our weak r-squared value, indicates that this relationship cannot be well described with this model and that, perhaps, it is not a strong relationship to analyze.
